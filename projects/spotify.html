Spotify "Your Library" Redesign

Integrating old functionality and new, re-imagined desktop features into a modern, mobile interface.

Overview

Spotify has changed the way music is consumed by millions of people worldwide. The audio streaming service is built on providing simplified access to a vast library of artists and songs, with its primary vehicle being mobile devices; as of 2018, GlobalWebIndex has reported an average 60% mobile usage vs 42% on desktop. The core appeal and value of Spotify has remained the same: efficiently accessing, organizing, and discovering music.

A June 2019 update to the Your Library portion of the mobile app nixed several key features, compromising this core appeal and value. In this redesign, I analyzed the changes and subsequent user responses, identified and reintegrated necessary components, and incorporated desktop-specific features onto the mobile platform.

Timeline

1 week (Dec 2019)

Role

UX Designer/Researcher

Tools

Facing the Music

The focus of this redesign was on the music portion of Spotify; despite the platforms recent expansion into podcasts, the platform's cornerstone is its music service. From Q3 2019 reportings, only 14% of monthly active users listen to podcasts. The redesign does not take away from the podcast experience, rather improves upon and fixes issues with the flawed music system. Additionally, while I based my redesign and research on the Android application which I had most access to, additional platform research found no significant difference between the iOS and Android version and issues and solutions are universal across the two operating systems.

Music over Podcasts

Initial Reactions

When the update hit devices in June 2019, I enjoyed the refreshed interface of “Your Library”; its new look and feel was sleek and modern. However, moving past the shiny, new paint-job revealed gutted functionality. A brief search about the update shows thousands of complaints from users, angrily demanding their old Spotify back. Many users, including myself, were forced to change the way we used the application and lost our personalized libraries.

3 goals starting the redesign:

Identify critical changed features

Integrate identified features into new interface

Develop and incorporate new features

Research and immersion

To familiarize myself with the pre-update Spotify, I rolled back the application on my device and spent a few days using the platform. I compared my experience between versions to identified what had been changed, and how it impacted my experience and use. I also combed through users responses and complaints from various channels, including Spotify’s own support forum. Often times the loudest voices are not always representative of the majority; therefore, to gain a more holistic perspective, I spoke with various Spotify users not only about the the update, but how they use Spotify everyday. A consistent theme stood out from the forum posts and user interviews:

organize and view content in a way meaningful to wide range of users

Problems and solutions

Saving songs

A distinct and nuanced annoyance I had about the update was the change from "adding" to "liking" songs. All liked songs are now complied in a playlist, but songs added to other playlist are not automatically liked, meaning they are not saved in a stable and permanent fashion, facilitating the potential “loss” of songs if a playlist is deleted.

The redesign reintroduced the Songs section to Your Library, containing all songs users have saved, whether from adding to a playlist or manually saving. The “liking” system and the “Liked Songs” playlist was kept to both nudge users to create their own playlist, and as another point of reference for Spotify's recommendation algorithm,

Following Artists

Following the update, users now must "follow" every artist if they wish to see their works under the Artists section. This change introduced unnecessary friction to organize and view works by artists, something that was done automatically in the past.

Spotify has commented that following certain artists better tailors recommendations, and notifications of artists’ activities. It would be detrimental to scrap the feature entirely, but rather than forcing users to follow every artists to see their saved works, every artist with songs saved in users’ library is displayed, and artists users choose to follow can be found with the new "following" filter. The redesign allows for a smarter recommendation algorithm, while respecting users time and freedom.

Select works from albums

The update removed the ability to save and play select songs by their albums, much like the artists section. Now, users must "like" a whole album for it to appear in this section and can only play whole albums.

The redesigned platform emulates the pre-update system, where all song in a user's library are included and organized into their respective albums. Selecting an album shows the saved songs from that album, with clear label notifying users of the selected works. If users wish to view the whole album, they can access the overflow menu in top right corner, and select the "Go to full album" option listed.

Custom playlist order

With over 4 billion playlists on the streaming service and many users creating 10+ playlists, it is imperative to provide efficient organization. On the desktop application, users are able to arrange their playlists in a custom order. This custom order is viewable but uneditable on mobile applications. This desktop exclusivity makes little sense to mobile-centric platform.

The redesign introduces a simple method for mobile rearrangement. Emulating how Spotify has implemented rearranging queued songs, while users are in the custom order view, they can drag each playlist into their desired order using the three bars on the right of every playlist.

Listening history

Like the custom ordering of playlists, listening history is only available on desktop. This oversight would be not as egregious if history between devices was synced, but it's not; the history on the desktop platform is desktop specific, and does not reflect activity from the mobile application.

This lack of synchronization could be remedied in the backend, but a better solution would be to also include listening history on the mobile platform. The redesign mirrors the desktop location of listening history, and has incorporated the much needed feature as a second tab within the queue feature.

Reflection

Spotify has undoubtedly changed the way a generation consumes music. However, in recent pursuits of prettier interfaces, the company has negatively impacted the core experience and value offered by the audio platform. The June 2019 update restricted users and stripped away many vital, organizational features. In this redesign, I hope to recapture the functionality and freedom of the past and crucial desktop features into the mobile application, while packaging it all in the same, sleek look and feel of the new Spotify.

Working within existing system

While working on this project, I focused on maintaining the look and feel of an existing product. I wanted the redesign to still feel like Spotify, and took meticulous care work within the company's existing design system. New features like the drag bars for the custom playlist and the listening history were designed to be consistent with previous implementations of similar Spotify features.

Research and removing self

The value of doing user research and understanding use cases was reaffirmed throughout this project, as my experience with the platform was not necessarily true for everyone. I constantly found myself surprised at the different ways everyone enjoyed the same platform, and the importance of not taking my own experiences to be indicative was very clear.

Future steps

Given more time and access to users, I would love to test the redesign with actual users and see if the resigned application both solves the identified issues, and does not introduce new problems. With Spotify's new interest in podcasts, I would also like to examine that side of the platform. As for other features, I believe that the scrolling system needs to be reworked as navigating through extensive libraries is time consuming without some form of quick alphabetical scrolling.
