OpenStax Internship

My experience as a UX Design Intern at an educational nonprofit, and developing an end-to-end solution.

Overview

During summer 2019, I had my first professional design experience as a UX design intern at OpenStax — an nonprofit, ed-tech initiative based out of Rice University. The organization provides free and low-cost educational resources (online textbooks and courseware) to millions of students worldwide. As of the end of 2019, 3 million students use OpenStax's free textbooks, and since 2012, the organization has saved 9 million students, $830 million. In my 12 weeks there, I gained hands-on experience working with the Agile development cycle, and collaborated with the UX team to research, design, and test various features across OpenStax products. I also worked with a team of interns to develop a new feature for enhancing student notetaking capabilities.

Learning about the process

After onboarding, my first 2 weeks were spent learning about the Agile software development process. I spoke with every department, from developers to upper management, about their role in creating and maintaining OpenStax products. I developed an understanding of the product’s lifecycle from its initial research and ideation, to its deployment and marketing. I learned exactly how UX designers contributed to and collaborated with stakeholders.

Day to day

I worked with the UX team to test and redesign the log-in/sign-up flow on OpenStax Tutor (an adaptive courseware utilizing cognitive science principles and machine learning), and on the OpenStax website itself. I conducted meticulous competitive analyses of flows across various other competitors’ platforms to catalog and analyze best practices when redesigning our user flows. Additionally, I conducted user testing of the redesigned user flows on usertesting.com to determine pain points and opportunities for improvement.

While at OpenStax, teams of interns were tasked with creating a new feature or product for either end users, or internal teams. Research

My research started with academic papers about student performance based on varying study habits and notetaking strategies. Surprisingly, studies have suggested that while highlighting is a universal and ubiquitous tool, the technique in practice has poor utility and offers inconsistent boosts to student performance. Often overused and misused, highlighting is a tool many students are all too familiar with, but correct use has never taught. Unsurprisingly, notetaking ranks as one of the most efficient and effective tools for students. The format of notes has another significant, compounding effect on student performance, with matrix and Cornell style notes outshining traditional outline formats.

I also interviewed several college students of varying ages and majors from Rice University. We reviewed their notetaking habits, utilization of highlighting, and preferences between online and physical tools. I also gathered and analyzed their notes to find any common patterns of behavior and strategies.

Solution

OpenNotes was our concept for an online notetaking platform built adjacent to the textbook experience. It is opened side by side with the text, enabling seamless and low-friction use.

Textbook reader with OpenNotes button on right side
Opening OpenNotes enters split view

The feature is focused on notetaking and improving student learning; unnecessary word processing features like fonts, font sizes, margins, and styles were removed. Instead, all of OpenNotes' provided tools are meant to aid students in taking better notes and reviewing for tests.

Highlighter tool
Pen tool

Using the tagging tool, users are able to mark notes with meaningful icons. If a topic is important, they can mark that section in their notes with an exclamation, or if topic is particularly tough, students can designate that section for review with glasses. By using these symbols, students can actively assess the notes they are taking, and provide meaningful, visual indications for themselves.

Tagging tool with meaningful icons

The layout of the notes into cards helps students organize and group material into smaller, more understandable sections. These cards or sections can be collapsed during review for a comprehensive overview of the material. When used in conjunction with the tagging tool, students would be able to efficiently recognize important or challenging topics, and selectively expand those notes for review.

Expanded notes
Notes can be partially expanded

OpenNotes also provides a way for students to create alternative note formats, like Cornell or Matrix style notes, which our research had indicated boosted student performance. A feature concept for Cornell notes was explored that would allow students to blur the definition side of their notes for self-testing, a proven and high utility study strategy.

Reflection

My time at OpenStax had been an eye-opening experience that helped my understanding of UX and product design. I was able to put into practice the principles and tools of design that I had self-taught, but realized that I still had great room for improvement and growth.  In particular, the development process of OpenNotes and the challenges I faced have enable me to become a better designer.

Lessons learned

Not getting married to one idea or fixed path to a solution.

Our team was committed to developing a notetaking platform early on and did not explore other ideas enough.

Quality and depth in research is often more revealing than quantity.

During user research, I had focused on interviewing as many students as possible rather than digging deeper into behaviors and the whys; this led to shallow assumptions.

Consider the context of use for a new feature or product.

Users come to OpenStax for access to the free textbooks online. They already have a familiar notetaking tool, and it is extremely hard to convince users to switch.

Play to strengths and moats.

A better solution would have leveraged the value of the OpenStax platform and free textbooks. Rather than competing with existing and experienced competitors in the notetaking space, a new feature should enhance or build from the already popular online textbooks.

Future steps

The lessons I learned have enabled me to build and refine my own design process and thinking. A major guiding personal principle I have gained is to frequently take a step back and ask yourself why you are taking taking the steps you are. I am grateful for my time at OpenStax as a foundational moment in my career.
